Greetings, fellow cybersecurity enthusiasts and red teamers! I am Stuxnet8, and today I invite you to dive into the captivating world of PowerShell built-in scripts. In this blog post, we will explore why these native capabilities are my preferred choice for conducting successful red teaming operations. In the realm of red teaming, efficiency, stealth, and adaptability are crucial elements to achieve our objectives. We constantly seek tools that provide us with the utmost control over our environment while remaining undetected by vigilant defenders. While there is a vast array of external scripts and frameworks available at our disposal, I firmly believe that harnessing the power of PowerShell's built-in scripts holds unmatched advantages.

Why, you might ask? Well, let me shed light on three compelling reasons:

1. Native Integration and Accessibility: PowerShell, an incredibly versatile scripting language developed by Microsoft, is natively integrated into modern Windows operating systems. This integration means that PowerShell scripts can seamlessly interact with the underlying system components, granting red teamers unparalleled access to a vast range of functionalities. By relying on built-in scripts, we eliminate dependencies on external tools, mitigating the risk of detection and raising our chances of success during engagements.

2. Evasion and Covert Operations: The PowerShell scripting environment offers an extensive arsenal of techniques that enable us to blend into the system's natural operations. Built-in scripts empower red teamers to execute operations with minimal footprint and evade traditional security measures. By exploiting PowerShell's inherent capabilities, we can effectively bypass security solutions that focus on detecting specific external tools or known patterns, giving us a significant advantage when it comes to stealthy maneuvers.

3. Flexibility and Adaptability:One of the most valuable aspects of PowerShell built-in scripts is their unparalleled flexibility. As red teamers, we often face ever-evolving challenges and scenarios. With PowerShell's extensibility, we can rapidly adapt our scripts to meet changing requirements without the need to depend on external software updates or third-party libraries. This adaptability allows us to respond swiftly and effectively to shifting target environments, enabling us to maintain the upper hand during engagements

In conclusion, embracing PowerShell built-in scripts provides red teamers with a formidable toolkit that amplifies our capabilities, bolsters our stealth, and enhances our chances of success. By leveraging native integration, evasive techniques, and flexibility, we can unleash the true potential of PowerShell and navigate the intricate landscape of red teaming with finesse. Throughout this blog series, we will delve deeper into the inner workings of PowerShell built-in scripts, exploring their various applications, sharing invaluable tips and tricks, and demonstrating real-world scenarios where they shine brightest.

So fasten your seatbelts, dear readers, for we are about to embark on an enlightening journey that will equip you with the skills to outwit and outmaneuver even the most vigilant defenders. Together, let's harness the power of PowerShell to become the masters of our digital domain! Stay tuned for the upcoming posts, where we will dive headfirst into the world of PowerShell's built-in scripts and uncover their hidden gems. Until then, happy scripting, and may your red teaming adventures be ever triumphant! Yours digitally, Stuxnet8

Enumerate All Users With Specific Properties

There could be many properties that a user could have. If we want to filter out some properties in all users, we can use below code:

PS C:> ([ADSISearcher]"(&(objectClass=user)(samAccountType=805306368))").FindAll() | %{ $_.Properties["samaccountname"] }

This code will just display “samaccountname” as a result for all users.

Enumerate all users with a SPN

A service instance’s service principal name (SPN) is a unique identifier. Kerberos authentication uses SPNs to link a service instance to a service login account. This enables a client application to ask the service to authenticate an account even if it doesn’t know the account name. If we want to display all users with SPN then we can use below code:

Service principal names (SPNs) are attached to user and computer Active Directory (AD) objects; you can add, remove, or modify them at will. One way to manage SPNs is to use the ActiveDirectory PowerShell module. This module contains the Get-Ad* and Set-Ad* cmdlets capable of reading and writing SPNs on user and computer objects. For now I have one default user krbtgt with SPN so I will give another user also permission of SPN.

Here I will use some powershell-ad scripts to simplify my work and proceed with the enumeartion

get-aduser -identity zamea -properties serviceprincipalnames | select serviceprincipalnames

Screenshot:

example setting SPN to AD user zamea

Set-ADUser -Identity zamea -ServicePrincipalNames @{Add='HTTP/webserver'}

Now we need to check again for any SPN'S on the user

get-aduser -identity zamea -properties * | select serviceprincipalnames
